Meteolakes is on Android:

Meteolakes is an online platform for monitoring and forecasting the bio-phyiscal state of Swiss lakes. The web-application shares with the public some results of 3D hydrodynamic-biological simulations performed daily with the Delft3D modelling framework on a compute server. Additionally, it displays measurements from a field station with data connexion and remote sensing images downloaded from satellites in real-time.

This is a development version, please contact Damien Bouffard for any information regarding the system operations, 3D simulations, data assimilation or online platform.

Coupling Remote Sensing In Situ and Models (CORESIM, now Alplakes)

Like islands, lakes are not only unique geographical features, but also ecological concepts on their own. Their exceptional variability has been studied numerous times, yet no unique framework capable of reproducing the wide range of lake dynamics observed in various regions of the World has been formulated. Increasingly facing external pressures, inland waters adaptation and changes have to be understood and monitored efficiently in order to provide timely, scientifically credible, and policy-relevant environmental information. Such monitoring capabilities are of great importance for the management and surveillance of the necessary measures to prevent lakes deterioration, as stated by the EU Water Framework Directive, or the United Nations’ Post 2015 agenda. Those are only two among the numerous recent political actions aiming at sustainably securing the ecosystem services provided by lakes on a regional to global scale, using new approaches for water management tailored to local conditions.

Over the last decades, various research communities addressed this problem using different information sources, such as in-situ measurements, remote sensing observations and mathematical models. The current challenge is to couple those sources through adapted data assimilation algorithms. The coupling approach envisaged in the CORESIM project implies mutual feedback mechanisms between those three information sources; model simulations are improved through the assimilation of in-situ measurements and remotely sensed products, remote sensing image processing is improved through parametrization with in-situ measurements and forecasts of hydrodynamic and biological models. Finally, in-situ measurements achieve a better representativeness when carried out along instantaneous gradients known from remotely sensed products and model simulations. This combination aims at assisting stakeholders in evidence-based decision-making and the sustainable management of our lakes.

This is a project funded by ESA (PI: Damien Bouffard).

  • Experimental: Real-time remote sensing data assimilation with Ensemble Kalman Filtering using the OpenDA assimilation platform (Lake Geneva only).

Authors and biography

Dr. Theo Baracchini

Theo Baracchini is a hydrodynamics and data assimilation (DA) specialist and the founder of Meteolakes. He has a masters in environmental engineering and conducted a PhD at EPFL in the Physics of Aquatic Systems Laboratory (APHYS), during which he developed an innovative approach integrating different information sources with 3D hydrodynamic models using state of the art ensemble techniques and algorithms for uncertainty quantification and errors mitigation (published in several peer-reviewed journals, such as Water Research). He developed, managed and maintained the end-to-end operational system for monitoring and forecasting lake dynamics: Meteolakes. After his PhD, he worked as a postdoc at the Swiss Federal Institute of Aquatic Science and Technology (Eawag), where he further developed this operational platform. He is now the data assimilation and operational hydrology specialist at the Research Center on Alpine Environment (CREALP), and focuses on operational flood forecasting of the Rhone river.

Dr. Damien Bouffard

Damien Bouffard is leader of the aquatic physics lab. at Eawag. He holds a PhD from EPFL. After his PhD he worked in Canada with Leon Boegman (Queen’s U.), Ram Yerubandi (Environment Canada) and Joe Ackerman (Guelph U.) before returning to Switzerland as first assistant at EPFL with Alfred (Johny) Wüest. He has published over 80 peer-reviewed papers in aquatic physics and serves as Associate Editor for JGLR, AS and HESS. His program research is focused on two axes: (i) linking the small-scale turbulence to the large-scale forcing with today a special interest in convective processes in lakes, and (ii) aquatic physics as a tool to understand lake ecosystems.
